Thoughtstream Card — Design Mockups

Four layout approaches for the microblog feed card. Key problems to resolve, then use the generator panel to send feedback.

Current card problems "Microblog" sticker is redundant on the thoughts feed · No clickable link to the adventure/project context · Meta (type indicator, context label) appears before the body, burying the content · Fixed 2-column image grid regardless of count or aspect ratio · Timestamp buried in footer at 0.6rem mono · Long posts overflow without truncation
A — Minimal / Reading-first Strip everything non-essential. Date + location as a single subtle line at top. Body immediately below — no interruptions. Adventure link revealed as a dashed bottom strip only when a context is present. Images use a smart layout: 1 image fills the full width; 2 split 50/50; 3 use a 2+1 asymmetric grid. Long posts truncate at 4 lines with a "read more" link.

✓ Body is the first thing you read. Clean. Fast.
✓ Adventure link is clear but doesn't dominate posts that don't have one.
✗ Context is easy to miss on posts where it's important.

There's a concept called "identity foreclosure" — committing to an identity before you've had time to explore who you actually are. It's usually applied to teenagers, but I think it happens to adults all the time. We pick a lane — career, politics, personality type — and then spend enormous energy defending that lane rather than questioning it. The internet makes this worse. You build a brand around your beliefs, you attract a tribe that mirrors them back at you, and now the cost of changing your mind includes the social cost of admitting you were wrong publicly. So people stop changing their minds. Not because the evidence stopped being compelling, but because the identity became load-bearing.

B — Context Banner When a post belongs to an adventure or project, a full-width coloured banner anchors the top of the card — dark green for adventures, dark navy for projects. The banner is clickable. Posts without context get no banner, staying lightweight. Date and location sit just below it. Body is still the first text you read.

✓ Adventure link is unmissable and prominent.
✓ Colour-coding creates visual grouping when browsing a mixed feed.
✗ Dark banner is visually heavy — may dominate cards unnecessarily.
✗ Non-contextual posts look incomplete by comparison.

C — Social Header A compact header row: circular avatar + username + relative date. Source badge (FB / Telegram) sits inline. Context appears as a coloured chip right in the header — it's clickable, and it's the first thing after the byline. Body text follows immediately. Location shows as a small line above images if present. More familiar/social-network feel.

✓ Familiar pattern — easier to scan quickly.
✓ Context chip in the header feels natural and clickable without being intrusive.
✗ Avatar/username redundant — this is always Sophie's profile.
✗ More elements competing for attention in the top row.

D — Image-first / Magazine If a post has images, lead with them. A dark date+location strip sits directly below the image(s) — no separate header row needed. Body text follows. When a context is present, it appears as a boxed callout card below the body — harder to miss than a strip, and feels like an invitation to go deeper. Text-only posts get a compact date header line instead.

✓ Images feel editorial and intentional, not incidental.
✓ Adventure callout is generous and hard to miss.
✗ Text-only posts feel like a different component entirely.
✗ Callout box adds height — may feel heavy on short posts.
